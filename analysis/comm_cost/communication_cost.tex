\paragraph{Analytical bandwidth model.} We distinguish two independent
quantities: the number of federated data-holding clients $C$, which
sweeps across our experiments, and the number of SMPC computational
parties $P$, which we fix to $P=\smpcParties{}$ throughout. The two
parameters affect bandwidth differently: $C$ scales the federation
total (more clients, more independent uploads), whereas $P$ controls
the secret-sharing share factor $(P-1)$ and thus the per-client SMPC
overhead. Let $R$ denote the number of communication rounds,
$|\theta|$ the trainable parameter count of the fine-tuned model, $d$
the embedding dimension ($\texttt{embsize}=128$ in our configurations),
$n_q$ the query cell count, $n_r$ the per-client reference cell count,
$k$ the number of nearest neighbours, and $M$ the histogram grid
resolution. We assume \texttt{float32} tensors throughout, since
CrypTen's additive sharing preserves the plaintext dtype. Under
additive sharing, each secret share is the same size as the underlying
tensor and one share is sent to every other party, so an SMPC upload
of a tensor of $B$ bytes costs $(P-1)\cdot B$ bytes per sender,
independent of $C$. The plaintext FedAvg path of
\texttt{aggregator.FedAvg.aggregate\_plain} contributes $4|\theta|$
bytes upload + $4|\theta|$ bytes broadcast per client per round, while
\texttt{aggregate\_smpc} pushes the per-round upload to
$(P-1)\cdot 4|\theta|$ but leaves the broadcast plaintext; over $R$
rounds this gives the entries in Table~\ref{tab:comm_cost_ft}. The
federated reference-mapping path
(\texttt{embedder.federated\_reference\_map}) charges per pass: in the
plaintext mode each client returns $(n_q,k)$ float distances plus
$(n_q,k)$ SHA-256 hex identifiers ($64$ bytes each); the SMPC mode
adds, per client, $(P-1)\cdot$ additive shares of the local reference
matrix and global-index offsets, an analytical upper-bound heuristic
of $(P-1)\cdot 2\cdot 4\cdot n_q\cdot n_r\cdot k$ bytes for the online
Beaver-triple traffic consumed by the masked-min iterative comparisons
in \texttt{top\_k\_ind\_selection} alone (excluding the initial
encrypted distance matmul, the subsequent distance-extraction
multiplies in \texttt{top\_k\_encrypted\_distances}, and
coordinator-side global top-k aggregation), and the encrypted output
shape $(n_q,k)$. The federated binning protocol moves only summary
statistics: in plaintext, $B_i$ and $n_i$ for the weighted-average
mode, or the local histogram $H_i$ plus $\max_i$ and $n_i$ for the
histogram mode; SMPC multiplies the per-client cost by $(P-1)$ and the
federation total by an additional factor of $C$.

\paragraph{Simulated wall-clock benchmark.} All bandwidth numbers in
Tables~\ref{tab:comm_cost_ft}--\ref{tab:comm_cost_binning} are analytical because they are determined
by the protocol and not by the network. To capture the cryptographic
overhead component, we additionally time the same aggregation paths
under CrypTen running as a simulated MPC protocol on a single host. We
isolate the federated KNN step
(\texttt{top\_k\_encrypted\_distances} + \texttt{top\_k\_ind\_selection})
from the rest of the embedding pipeline by feeding it pre-computed
embeddings of the target shape so that the timings are interpretable
as pure SMPC cost; the same warm-up + median-of-N protocol is used
for fine-tuning (\texttt{FedAvg.aggregate\_plain} vs
\texttt{aggregate\_smpc}) and binning aggregation. Wall-clock times
therefore include encryption/decryption, fixed-point quantisation, and
CrypTen's protocol overhead, but exclude real network latency between
parties. Beaver-triple precomputation is treated as offline, in line
with the CrypTen default. A real multi-party deployment would add
latency on top of these numbers but would not change the analytical
byte counts.

\section*{Results}

\paragraph{Fine-tuning weight sharing scales linearly with $|\theta|$
under SMPC.} With $P=\smpcParties{}$ parties held constant, the
plaintext FedAvg/FedProx upload is $4|\theta|$ bytes per client per
round, whereas the SMPC upload is $(P-1)\cdot 4|\theta|=2\cdot 4|\theta|=8|\theta|$
bytes per round (with $P=\smpcParties{}$). Crucially, the SMPC per-client traffic is independent
of the federated client count $C$: adding more clients increases the
federation total proportionally to $C$ but leaves the per-client
share factor fixed at $(P-1)$. Over $R$ rounds the per-client SMPC
traffic is $\ftBytesRatioMax{}\times$ the plaintext total
(Figure~\ref{fig:comm_cost_ft}). Measured SMPC wall-clock overhead
reaches up to $\ftSmpcOverheadMax{}\times$ over plaintext across the
fine-tuning sweep (Table~\ref{tab:comm_cost_ft}).

\paragraph{Secure KNN is the dominant cryptographic cost.} The
federated reference-mapping pass is dominated by
\texttt{top\_k\_encrypted\_distances}, which iterates
$k$ encrypted \texttt{min} + \texttt{suppress\_argmin} reductions over
an $(n_q, n_r)$ distance matrix. The closed-form upper bound on Beaver-triple online traffic for the
masked-min loop in \texttt{top\_k\_ind\_selection} grows linearly with
the local reference size $n_r$ and with $k$
(Figure~\ref{fig:comm_cost_knn}). Across the synthetic
configurations in Table~\ref{tab:comm_cost_knn}, per-configuration crypto
overhead ranges from $10^3$--$10^4\times$ (median
$\knnSmpcOverheadMedian{}\times$; worst case
$\knnSmpcOverheadMax{}\times$). Figure~\ref{fig:comm_cost_wallclock}
summarises reference mapping by the ratio of aggregate median wall-clock
times ($\knnSmpcOverheadFig{}\times$), which differs from the median of
per-configuration ratios because overhead is computed row-wise. This
empirically confirms that the SMPC KNN step is the most expensive
component of the workflow and motivates both the round-count emphasis
in the main text and the multi-server-side hash-only fallback in
\texttt{compute\_local\_distances}.

\paragraph{Federated binning communication is negligible.} The binning
preprocessing step exchanges only summary statistics: for the
histogram modes this is a length-$M$ pooled histogram (typical
$M = 4096$, i.e. on the order of $16$\,KB per client even under SMPC),
and for the weighted-average modes the per-client quantile vector is
only $B-1 = 50$ floats. As reported in
Table~\ref{tab:comm_cost_binning}, the plaintext and SMPC variants of
fed-weight-avg and fed-hist differ by at most a factor of
$(P-1)$ on a base of a few tens of
kilobytes per client (independent of $C$), well below the per-round
cost of weight sharing on a single fine-tuning round, and they happen
exactly once at initialisation.

\section*{Limitations and caveats}

The wall-clock numbers are obtained with CrypTen running as a simulated
MPC protocol in a single process. They isolate the cryptographic
overhead of additive-sharing operations and CrypTen's fixed-point
arithmetic, but they do not measure network throughput, real
multi-party latency, GPU utilisation, or Beaver-triple precomputation
cost. The analytical bandwidth model is exact for CrypTen's additive
sharing under the assumption that each share equals the underlying
tensor in size; if a future revision of CrypTen switches to replicated
or honest-majority sharing schemes, the formulas in
Section~``Methods'' above would have to be updated and the CrypTen
version pinned in \texttt{requirements.txt} accordingly. The simulated
wall-clock numbers correspond to CrypTen's process configuration,
which may differ from the analytical party count $P$; we report the
party count explicitly on every table and figure so the two
assumptions can be reconciled. Finally, the broadcast of the global
model in \texttt{FedAnnotator.local\_update} remains in plaintext in
the current implementation; closing that gap would multiply the
per-round download cost by $(P-1)$ but does not affect the upload
trends shown in Figure~\ref{fig:comm_cost_ft}.
